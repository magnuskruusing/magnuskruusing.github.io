Berger’s travel camera

A similar camera was probably used for city views by the most famous photographer of old Reval (former name of Tallinn) - Charles Borchardt (1834-1892)

Museum of Photography TLM KF 364

1 - bellows | 2 - lens | 3 - matte glass | 4 - aperture wheel | 5 - lens plate screw | 6 - tripod screw hole

G. Berger's photo and optics company, who compiled such cameras, was the very first shop of photography opened in Reval (Tallinn). The shop was opened as early as 1863 at Raderstrasse No 270 (now Nunne Street 1).

This kind of light travel camera was designed for taking pictures of places and events. This particular camera is probably manufactured at the end of the 19th century. This camera was able to shoot on up to 18x24 cm glass plate negatives (glass plates with silver collodion or gelatin emulsion).

Instant photo camera

A camera patented by Jaan Ennula

1 - light-proof sleeve | 2 - reproduction support | 3 - opening for the lens | 4 - viewing window | 5 - focusing spar | 6 - matte glass

JAAN ENNULA (ca 1900-1945), a street photographer, built his own camera. His instant photo camera with 6 lenses was patented in 1924. With this camera, up to 6 different shots could be done on one photo paper. So, the client could get six photos – this was a great advantage compared to other street photographers.

The instant cameras were mainly used to photograph people. Painted backgrounds and requisites (goats, bears) were often used to attract customers.

The camera was built as if it were a miniature photo lab. The photographer took the image not on a film but on photo paper. The negatiive image was then developed and fixed inside the camera, in small baths. Through the window glazed with a red glass it was possible to observe the course of the development. After the fixation, a drawer was opened in the back of the camera to get the negative print out for a quick rinse. Then the negatiive print was fixed before the camera lens to take a positive repro photo of the negative print. The entire previous process was repeated and thus a positive photo print was obtained.

Peegelkaamera NIKON F

Peeter Tooming (1939-1997) used this camera for his art of photography

Museum of Photography, TLM KF 775

1 - shutter release | 2 - removable pentaprism (mirror system) | 3 - selftimer | 4 - film rewind knob | 5 - film winding knob | 6 - lens | 7 - tripod screw hole | 8 - focusing wheel | 9 - viewfinder

This ultra-popular 13 years in manufacturing camera was the first SLR camera (no separate lens for the viewfinder window) of the famous Japanese corporation Nikon. In 1959, when the camera came on sale, it was the world's most skillful SLR camera. The camera used 35mm film and combined the best solutions of a photo camera of that time. It offered many options for the photographer, including the possibility to change lenses.

NIKON F SLR cameras were manufactured until 1973, when the upgraded NIKON F2 came. The bayonet of NIKON SLR cameras (lens thread) has remained unchanged until today to support consistent usage.

Charles Borchardt (1834 - 1892)

Charles Borchardt (alias Carl Amandus Borchardt) was one of the most famous photographers in Tallinn in the 19th century. He photographed mainly portraits, groups and views of the town.

Already in 1853 he was temporarily working in Reval (the name of Tallinn at that time). In 1861, he opened his own photo studio in Lai Street opposite the Church of Oleviste and worked there successfully until the very end of his life. His photographs cover almost 40 years of Reval in the 19th century, depicting its residents and buildings.

Charles Borchardt was highly regarded among the citizens. He also received official recognition. In 1867, he won a high prize – the silver medal - in the Russian Ethnographic Exhibition of the Society of Nature in the Imperial University of Moscow. The prize was given for the ethnographic portrait series "Estonian Types" which depicted the local Estonians in 12 full-length portrait photographs (out of which 8 has preserved in Estonian public collections).

In the Museum of Photography, old photo albums hold many portrait photographs by Charles Borchard from different decades of 19th century. Also beautiful views of Reval (Tallinn) can be found in the format of cabinet card (approximate size 11x16 cm). The collection also holds the Baltic Railway album (a gift for the founder of the railway) and a pair of the surviving photographs of the high-prized "Estonian Types" series.

Jaan Ennula (ca. 1900-1945)

Jaan Ennula (alias Jaan Kärsmann) was the most important street-photographer in Estonia (a photographer working on streets for making instant portraits). He was an enterprising inventor with skillful hands, and also one of Estonia's longest-working street-photographer. Under the leadership of Jaan Ennula, the Estonian Instant Photography Association was started. The association defended the rights of street photographers in the 1930’s, which was definitely a matter of concern for the photo-studio owners of that time.

Jaan Ennula began to make instant portraits in Tallinn already in 1921 and worked here until the end of his life. His main place was Park Šnelli near the Nunne Street, and in the 1930’s he also worked at Park Kadriorg, near the tram stop. Jaan Ennula's fascinating work is preserved in the Museum of Photography.

Peeter Tooming (1939-1997)

Peeter Tooming was an innovative and hard-working film operator, photographer and writer. From 1961 to 1994, he worked as film operator in "Tallinnfilm", and in addition, in the times of Soviet Estonia and after regaining the independence of Estonian Republic, he made many modern photographic exhibitions and published books on Estonian photography.

Peeter Tooming explored the history of Estonian photography and laid the foundation for the Museum of Photography. His articles and photos have appeared in world-renowned journals of photography. Peeter Tooming's rich creation and collections are here in the Museum of Photography.
